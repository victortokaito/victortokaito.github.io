UCB Food Pantry: Designing to Promote Access

I began volunteering with the UCB Food Pantry in the summer of 2015—it served 500 students in the first year.

Since then, the number of students served by the food pantry has surged to reflect the number of students in need, largely because we have been able to normalize its use (we also developed an app to track inventory, ease the experience for volunteers as well as students, and publish total number of visits).

Our combined efforts with on-campus advocates have increased awareness and access to the pantry—it has since grown from its space in Stile Hall to MLK Student Union, where it now serves nearly 7000 students out of 42,000!

Rattlesneck: Addressing Biker Safety in Urban Settings

Roads are not safe for cyclists. According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, 98% of average annual cyclist deaths were caused by vehicles in recent years. The organization also estimates that 55,000 American cyclists are injured in car accidents every year – and given that accidents are often underreported – this number may only represent 10% of all cyclist injuries.

I love biking, but some days I feel I'm placing my life in danger as I ride onto the street. This project was explored based on my own concerns as a biker, and further developed by way of surveys, user interviews, and usability tests [(n=50), (n=5), (n=15)]. Sifting through the data, I defined the most common problem area with a team and surveyed other issues bikers were facing. In our research, we also studied what bikers are currently using to augment their awareness and make themselves more visible.

From 50 surveys, we learned half of our participants had been in bike accidents involving cars. Surprisingly, 90% of them also believed that an improved sense of awareness either on the part of the biker or the driver would have prevented their accidents.

In an effort to enforce the "3 feet passing law" in CA, we developed Rattlesneck as a wearable device that senses approaching cars via ultrasonic sensors, which vibrate to warn the user, and flashes LED lights to warn drivers.

PenGrip: Addressing Wrist Pain in Digital Artists

Pixar graphic artists voiced concerns to our Professor (who serves as Director of UCSF/UCB Ergonomics Research & Graduate Training). Our team then, took on this task analysis with the promise of visiting Pixar Studios for contextual inquiry ☺️.

The subset of graphic artists we focus on, digital artists, use a tablet and a tablet pen to draw on digital software. In our research, we found a 43% incidence rate of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) among a group of graphic artists, compared to no incidences of CTS in other non-graphic artist workers in the same department.

Further, we conducted surveys and user interviews (n=29, n=5) to suss out their frustrations with work-related fatigue. Among 29 respondents, 22 reported having experienced pain of some kind. Of these 22, 12 experienced wrist pain specifically, with shoulder, lower back, and neck closely following. The most common reasons for RSI injuries: repetitive strain, excessive gripping, and poor posture held for too long without a break.

With an artist participant, I conducted a task analysis using the Revised Strain Index to evaluate the severity of this task and risk of developing CTS. After estimating risk with Kinovea, our team discussed potential interventions. We concluded that reducing the “intensity of exertions” and "augmenting grip width" was the most effective way of addressing the development of CTS. We presented our potential solutions to the focus group. The culmination of their preferences resulted in a redesign of a pen case for artists using tablet pens.

Echo: Exploring Echo Chambers

Exploring Political Echo Chambers

‍
While numerous studies examine the relationship between social media and political behavior, scholars continue to debate its effects on political polarization. To inform this debate, our multi-disciplinary team measures the effect of social media echo chambers on public opinion (e.g. Twitter). Our discovery that peoples' views on political issues converge on the general opinion of media leaders within their echo chamber gives nuance to the traditional idea. Whereas it was previously believed that political echo chambers polarize from right to left, we find that echo chambers sort folks into fractioned factions. Though it's clear that people heavily anchor their views to the media they read, this discovery suggests that with an increased number of independent media, the political spectrum has potential to balance itself out.

I thrive in design research capacities. I especially enjoy facilitating concept exploration––Here, I worked with a group to explore the context of echo chambers. I believe it's essential to define pains early on. Before even discussing potential solutions, the full context of the situation should be illuminated so that we can arm ourselves with pertinent knowledge. Our affinity mapping in this phase grounded our later decisions in designing our research.

Gathering evidence isn't optional. Our assumptions and thus, our design decisions are grounded in our personal perspectives so it's critical that we seek out the knowledge and experiences of folks on the ground.

After developing an awareness of the field, we began discussion that naturally headed towards what obstructs communication. Then, we ideated around current systems that contribute to confirmation bias and echo chambers (i.e. tech companies that bombard you with your own interests). We define our goal to increase communication across the political spectrum, while reducing a tribalism, and a tendency to seek out ideas that self-affirm.
