Rattlesneck: Addressing Biker Safety in Urban Settings

Read More
Hide
Roads are not safe for cyclists.

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration estimates that 55,000 cyclists were injured in car accidents last year––Of even greater concern is that the number of fatal bike accidents has increased over the last decade.

Further, these statistics show that the bulk of these fatal accidents occur in urban areas. They are particularly high in dense, populous cities whereas the safest areas for bikers are those in smaller cities with less metropolitan bustle. This suggests that the busy-ness of large cities contributes to neglectful behavior on both the part of drivers, cyclists, and runners.

Addressing this lack of awareness became the driving force of our proposed solution.

Sifting through the data, I defined the most common problem area with my team and surveyed other issues bikers were facing. In our research, we also studied what accessories bikers are currently using to augment their awareness and make themselves more visible.

From 52 Qualtrics surveys, we learned half of our participants had been in bike accidents involving cars. Surprisingly, nearly 90% of them also believed that an improved sense of awareness either on the part of the biker or the driver would have prevented their accidents.

40% of these cyclists also suggested finding ways to apply the "three-foot passing law,” which requires motorists to give cyclists at least three-feet of clearance when passing them. In an effort to keep drivers & bikers alert (as well as to help enforce the "3 feet passing law”), we developed “Rattlesneck” as an initial prototype to sense approaching cars via ultrasonic sensors, vibrate to warn the user, and flashes LED lights to warn drivers.

‍

Measuring Success:

To test the prototype, we developed a task-based scenario for 15 users to measure their experience in terms of key qualities (via post-test Likert scale survey):

  • Functionality (can users feel the difference in vibrations from 3, 3-5, and 5+ ft?)
  • Feeling of safety (perception of safety)
  • Awareness of other party (preventative)
  • Bikers - increased visibility to drivers 
  • Drivers - increased awareness of bikers(via post-test survey)

‍

I love biking, but some days I feel I'm placing my life in danger as I ride onto the street. This project was explored based on my own concerns as a biker, and further developed by way of surveys, user interviews, and usability tests [(n=52), (n=5), (n=15)]. We are continuing this research in Spring 2020 with increased funding, new sensors, and in-depth questions. We continue to ask how we might best improve the experience of biking and  feeling safe.

UCB Food Pantry: Designing to Promote Access

Read More
Hide
The need to address food insecurity among students is real.

I began volunteering with the UCB Food Pantry in the summer of 2015—it served only 500 students in the first year.

The volunteer team at the time, consisted primarily of 5 students. Among us were student parents, formerly incarcerated students, and re-entry students.  

Having each experienced the myriad effects of going to a Bay Area university, we knew that we were not alone in our feelings of food scarcity—We proposed researching how we might extend our reach in the UC Berkeley community. According to a 2016 google survey we released (n = 48), 42% of students had expressed experiencing food insecurity at some point in their time at UCB. However, only 6% of these students had made use of the student food pantry.  

We began to ask questions:

  • ‍What does the population we are serving look like?
  • Are we reaching everyone who fits this need?
  • Who might we be missing?

In unstructured interviews, we uncovered a stigma around using the services of a food pantry (especially for students).

Our continued student advocacy at the 2015 Student of Color Conference urged UC regents to endorse a UC-wide effort to gather and analyze data on food and housing insecurity. Ultimately, our efforts (alongside campus advocates) led to a UC-based study that confirmed this need citing “44 percent of undergrads and 26 percent of grad students in UC schools experience food insecurity at some point in their academic career”.

Since then, the number of students served by the food pantry has surged to reflect the number of students in need, largely because we have been able to let students know that they were not alone.

To help normalize its use, we collaborated with volunteer computer science students in 2018 to develop an android app which tracks inventory, and eases the experience for volunteers as well as students. When choosing which operating system to develop for, we found that although iOS is more dominant in our school and the US in general, the bulk of pantry visitors (65%) were android users. My future plans are to conduct usability tests, and further explore how we might improve the experience of students.

‍

Our combined efforts with on-campus advocates have increased awareness and access to the pantry—it has since grown from its space in Stiles Hall to MLK Student Union, where it now serves just shy of 7000 students (out of 42,000 on campus)!

PenGrip: Addressing Wrist Pain in Digital Artists

Read More
Hide

Pixar graphic artists voiced concerns to our Professor (who serves as Director of UCSF/UCB Ergonomics Research & Graduate Training). Our team then, took on this task analysis with the promise of visiting Pixar Studios for contextual inquiry ☺️.

The subset of graphic artists we focus on, digital artists, use a tablet and a tablet pen to draw on digital software. In our research, we found a 43% incidence rate of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) among a group of graphic artists, compared to no incidences of CTS in other non-graphic artist workers in the same department.

Further, we conducted surveys and user interviews (n=29, n=5) to suss out their frustrations with work-related fatigue. Among 29 respondents, 22 reported having experienced pain of some kind. Of these 22, 12 experienced wrist pain specifically, with shoulder, lower back, and neck closely following. The most common reasons for RSI injuries: repetitive strain, excessive gripping, and poor posture held for too long without a break.

I conducted a task analysis with a graphic artist, using the Revised Strain Index to evaluate the severity of this task and risk of developing CTS. After estimating risk with Kinovea, our team discussed potential interventions. We concluded that reducing the “intensity of exertions” and "augmenting grip width" was the most effective way of addressing the development of CTS. We presented our potential solutions to the focus group. The culmination of their preferences resulted in a redesign of a pen case for artists using tablet pens.

Design to Reduce Error

Design Evaluation: 

With our main goal achieved of increasing the pen's grip width to reduce the overall 'pinch grip force", we looked on to the second-most desired product design change: addition of hotkeys. In the design evaluation of our penultimate prototype, we assessed modifications that would clarify how the prototype can be used while reducing likelihood of error.

Possible Errors: 

  • Slip: Trying to use hotkeys function before turning it on. Action error.
  • Lapse: Forgetting to use the hotkeys. Cognition error. 
  • Violation: Ignoring the suggestions of the grip sensor. Cognition error. 

Modifications to address errors: 

  • Adding a feature to hold down the hotkey button for three seconds to turn them on/off. The lights will flash three times in yellow, then once in green/red to indicate that three seconds have passed and the hotkeys have been turned on (green) or off (red). 
  • As a redundancy, both the top and bottom buttons will work to turn it on. This avoids a user blaming themself for not remembering which button correctly turns on the hot keys.
  • Adding a feature to also pair the hotkey function to the iPad via Bluetooth. Similar to turning the hotkeys on/off, hold down either hotkey for 5 seconds until it goes into pairing mode.

OnePlug: Developing A Child-Safe Outlet Adapter

Read More
Hide

Everyday in the U.S. alone, 7 children are hospitalized because of hazardous electrical outlets.

Today’s electrical outlet designs and have not changed in nearly a century. That’s why our team developed a safe, tamper-proof, and magnetic outlet adapter to address the dangers that traditional outlets continue to hold for families in the US. Our goal is to improve the modern “electrical outlet” in order to make the current system safer for children, while simultaneously promoting accessibility for the physically and visually-impaired population.

‍

‍

Out of over 90 teams and 700 students, our team was selected by a panel of judges (comprised of patent lawyers and venture capitalists) for the largest cash-value prize at the European Innovation Academy in 2017. Our award included a patent scholarship by Nixon Peabody LLC. - a global 100 law firm- and as such was one of the winning teams at the innovation academy. Our award also included one year of legal counsel by Nixon Peabody.

‍

When all you have is a hammer, every problem starts to look like a nail.

The desire to start a business has created a norm where enterprise solutions exceed the number of problems with validated needs. This results in businesses forcing products into already-saturated markets. For this project, we did not struggle searching for solutions to problems that didn't exist––Instead, we started with a pain point and allowed that to guide our design process.

Distinct to our design-centered team was a determination not on finding the right solution, but in discovering a worthy problem. I worked on this design research project with an MBA from Oslo, Norway, an industrial designer from Bangalore, India, an electromechanical engineering Ph.D from Torino, Italy, and a chemical engineer from Massachusetts. In our exploratory research, we found parents worried about the safety of their children when it came to hazard-prone electrical outlets. Our hopes to develop a tangible solution to real world issues stems from our passion for evidence-driven, effective design.

HandSolo: Designing to Enable Joy

Read More
Hide

I was invited to help a UCB Mechanical Engineering student lead usability tests for this project.

HandSolo is a controller system concept designed for folks with full use of one hand.

The goal for this study was to refine and improve the electronics and form factor based on user-testing. I was charged with designing a task-based research study and recruiting participants.

‍

‍

With the help of the graduate student, I conducted task-based user research studies which ultimately informed the prototype's updates.

In this set of user research studies, I have users' think aloud thought processes as they explore the placement of triggers & buttons. As users play video games with the controller, I observed movements, noticing errors to suggest improvements, and integrate key findings into a final prototype.

In research, we have to get creative with the ways in which we find and recruit participants who match the specific psychographics/demographics to validate the need. Ultimately, I found the challenge of recruiting users with specific anthropometrics exceedingly difficult. The goal then becomes finding workarounds (like having users use only one hand) and maintaining valid data.

Echo: Exploring Echo Chambers

Read More
Hide

Exploring Political Echo Chambers

‍
While numerous studies examine the relationship between social media and political behavior, scholars continue to debate its effects on political polarization. To inform this debate, our multi-disciplinary team measures the effect of social media echo chambers on public opinion (e.g. Twitter). Our discovery that peoples' views on political issues converge on the general opinion of media leaders within their echo chamber gives nuance to the traditional idea. Whereas it was previously believed that political echo chambers polarize from right to left, we find that echo chambers sort folks into fractioned factions. Though it's clear that people heavily anchor their views to the media they read, this discovery suggests that with an increased number of independent media, the political spectrum has potential to balance itself out.

I thrive in design research capacities. I especially enjoy facilitating concept exploration––Here, I worked with a group to explore the context of echo chambers. I believe it's essential to define pains early on. Before even discussing potential solutions, the full context of the situation should be illuminated so that we can arm ourselves with pertinent knowledge. Our affinity mapping in this phase grounded our later decisions in designing our research.

Gathering evidence isn't optional. Our assumptions and thus, our design decisions are grounded in our personal perspectives so it's critical that we seek out the knowledge and experiences of folks on the ground.

After developing an awareness of the field, we began discussion that naturally headed towards what obstructs communication. Then, we ideated around current systems that contribute to confirmation bias and echo chambers (i.e. tech companies that bombard you with your own interests). We define our goal to increase communication across the political spectrum, while reducing a tribalism, and a tendency to seek out ideas that self-affirm.
